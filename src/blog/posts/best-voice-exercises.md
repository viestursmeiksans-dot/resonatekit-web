---
title: "The Best Voice Exercises for Speaking (That Work Fast)"
description: "Resonance, breathing and diction exercises to make your voice deeper, clearer and more powerful — plus the actor's secret: turning everyday speech into daily practice. From theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">"How am I supposed to find time every day for voice exercises?" The answer is: <strong>every time you speak.</strong> Your daily communication is the best practice platform there is. Below are the most effective resonance, breathing and diction exercises — plus the one mindset shift that turns ordinary conversation into voice training.</p>

<p>A deeper, well-placed voice isn't just luck. Research where people read the <em>same</em> text shows that lower, warmer voices are perceived as more competent, intelligent and trustworthy — which is why you rarely hear a weather forecast read in a high, squeaky voice. Your voice can become your formula for success and your strongest tool for persuasion — or, left unattended, the reason connections don't form. The good news: it's trainable.</p>

<h2>Resonance exercises</h2>
<p>You want sound to resonate in your <strong>body</strong>, not get stuck in your head, nose or throat. Stand with your back flat against a wall and "speak into your back," as if your mouth were between your shoulder blades — you should feel the wall vibrate. Start with a simple hum on "mmm," then add syllables:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Open vowels: "ooo, aaa, uuu"</li>
  <li>Voiced syllables: "ga-ga-ga, ba-ba-ba, da-da-da"</li>
</ul>
<p>Combine this with a <strong>physical</strong> move: curl into a tight ball on the floor, then slowly open out to a wide, upright posture while humming "aaa" — and feel the voice open and broaden with the body. Voice forms according to your inner state, so cultivate the sense of space, power and openness: feel that you fill the whole room, that you're large and broad, and your voice will follow.</p>

<h2>Breathing exercises (the foundation of everything)</h2>
<p>Most voice problems start with breath. Under nerves, speakers literally "forget to breathe," and oxygen is exactly what calms anxiety — without breath control, good results are impossible. Learn to exhale first:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Exhale fully</strong> — squeeze the stale air out of every corner of your lungs, then hold for 3 seconds.</li>
  <li><strong>Inhale from the lower belly up</strong>, gradually filling your whole body like a barrel. Picture your body as a vessel you're filling. Hold for 3 seconds.</li>
  <li><strong>Exhale long and slow.</strong></li>
</ol>
<p>This is both the foundation of inner calm and the "spring" your voice pushes off. It's also one of the most reliable ways to manage <a href="/blog/overcome-stage-fright/">stage fright</a>.</p>

<h2>Diction &amp; articulation exercises</h2>
<p>Fifteen minutes a day for two weeks visibly strengthens your mouth muscles. Run vowels with a wide-open, exaggerated mouth, then pair each consonant with every vowel — "ma-me-mi-mo-mu, na-ne-ni-no-nu…" — gradually increasing speed as the sounds stay crisp.</p>
<h3>Tongue twisters</h3>
<p>Tongue twisters are superb articulation tools. Use ones in your own language, and start <strong>slowly</strong> — be aware of how you form each sound and never drop final syllables — before building up speed. Classic English examples to drill specific sounds: <em>"Red lorry, yellow lorry,"</em> <em>"She sells seashells by the seashore,"</em> and <em>"Unique New York."</em> Treat each one as training for a specific sound.</p>

<h2>Your emotional state shapes your voice</h2>
<p>Voice isn't only physiology. You can't build a powerful, resonant voice while you feel flat and low — the voice is a mirror that shows exactly how you feel. Working on your inner state and your <a href="/blog/how-to-develop-charisma/">charisma</a> changes how you sound.</p>

<h2>The actor's secret: erase the line between "speech" and "everyday talk"</h2>
<p>The actors with the most beautiful voices share one habit — there's <strong>no difference</strong> between how they speak on stage and how they speak at the breakfast table. Anyone can borrow this: be fully present in every bit of everyday communication, consciously choosing words, sounds and tone, savouring each word and giving it the right mood. Start hearing yourself from the outside on the phone, over breakfast, anywhere — and feel the small joy when a single well-chosen word lands your idea more vividly. That breaks the wall between "prepared speeches" and daily life, which is where lasting improvement comes from.</p>

<h2>Speak in CAPITAL LETTERS</h2>
<p>In rehearsals I often tell actors: "Say <em>this</em> phrase in capital letters." It means do everything to make the phrase more important and intense — and they slow down, savour each letter and sound, and it becomes a completely different line. Clearly, intensely spoken words capture attention: the listener instantly knows <em>this</em> one matters and they need to hear it.</p>

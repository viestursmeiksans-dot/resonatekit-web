<p class="lead">Working in another country isn't just a job and a payslip — it's <strong>building relationships, finding collaborators and growing a professional network</strong>. Yet basic communication and interpersonal mistakes are what quietly spoil it for most people. Below are the most common communication errors people make when working abroad or inside an international team — and the cleanest ways to fix them.</p>

<h2>Language barriers and how to clear them</h2>
<p>The most obvious challenge is the language barrier. The fix starts before you arrive: invest real time and effort into the language while you're still preparing, and keep going once you're there. But the day-to-day discipline matters even more.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Ask people to repeat themselves.</strong> If you didn't catch something, ask for it again. The worst thing you can do is stay silent, freeze, or answer a question you weren't asked. Nobody respects a confident guess that misses the point.</li>
  <li><strong>Say you're not a local.</strong> A simple "I'm still learning the language" almost always triggers empathy — people will slow down and re-explain in simpler words. There's no shame in it.</li>
  <li><strong>Talk to locals as much as you can.</strong> You won't absorb the language if you only speak your native tongue with people from back home. The single most effective course is spending a few months in the local environment with no fallback to your own language.</li>
</ul>
<p>For the underlying skill of explaining yourself clearly under pressure, the same habits that help <a href="/blog/communication-tips-for-introverts/">quieter people speak up</a> apply here too.</p>

<h2>Non-verbal communication changes across cultures</h2>
<p>The meaning of your body language shifts enormously from one culture to the next. A gesture that reads as warm or neutral in one place can land as rude or even offensive in another. Three things to watch closely:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Personal distance.</strong> How much space people keep during a conversation varies a lot. In the US, Canada and Scandinavia, people hold a comfortable arm's-length gap. In Spain, Italy and other Mediterranean cultures, conversations happen far closer in — and stepping back can read as cold.</li>
  <li><strong>Eye contact.</strong> In most Western cultures, steady eye contact signals respect and attention. In parts of East Asia, prolonged eye contact can feel intrusive or even aggressive. Calibrate to the room.</li>
  <li><strong>Silence.</strong> In the West, silence is often read as awkward — hence the whole institution of "small talk," which mostly exists to fill the gaps. In many Eastern cultures, silence reads as thoughtfulness and consideration, a positive signal when you're building trust.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Time is understood differently too</h2>
<p>How a culture treats time directly affects how professional you appear and how well you build partnerships. A few patterns worth knowing:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Deadlines.</strong> In most Western cultures, deadlines are close to sacred — you keep them, full stop. In many cultures with a more relaxed relationship to time, a deadline can slip or move without anyone treating it as a crisis. Neither view is wrong; just know which room you're in.</li>
  <li><strong>Present vs. future orientation.</strong> Some cultures live in the present — they value the current moment, adapt quickly and stay open to spontaneous ideas. Others are strongly future-oriented (think the US or Germany), where everything is planned ahead and the plan is followed closely.</li>
  <li><strong>Meetings and one-to-one talk.</strong> In relationship-first cultures, a work meeting may open with a long stretch of conversation that has nothing to do with business — and that's normal, it's how trust gets built. In efficiency-first cultures, every minute counts and people get straight to the point.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treat it as the opportunity it is</h2>
<p>Working abroad can open doors for years to come — but only if you remember that you're moving between genuinely different cultures, not just different offices. Grasp the broad differences in how people handle distance, eye contact, silence and time, and you'll handle roughly 70% of situations correctly on instinct.</p>
<p>For the remaining 30%, the rule is simple: <strong>watch and mirror</strong>. Pay attention to how your colleagues and partners behave, then quietly match their conduct. Curiosity beats assumption every time.</p>

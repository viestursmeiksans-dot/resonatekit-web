title: "How to Be a Great Public Speaker: A Director's Guide"

---
<p class="lead">Public speaking is one of the most powerful instruments for personal growth. It bundles together several skills that make you a person worth listening to — and, done well, it can change minds and, through them, the world.</p>

<p>Three things make it so valuable. First, it forces you to <strong>organise your thinking</strong> clearly — turning what you understand into something an audience understands is one of the most prized modern skills. Second, it's an <strong>instrument of inspiration</strong>: from "I Have a Dream" to a persuasive sales pitch, speech is how we move people. Third, it's <strong>brave territory</strong> — the audience can feel like a many-headed giant, and the speaker like David facing Goliath. Everyone feels that anxiety; overcoming it is exactly what makes you more confident.</p>

<h2>Know your genre before you pick a topic</h2>
<p>Before you think about <em>what</em> to say, get clear on the task. There are four main public-speaking genres:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Persuasion or sales speeches</strong> — hugely common; the art of persuasion is one of today's most important skills.</li>
  <li><strong>Ceremonial speeches</strong> — toasts, eulogies, congratulations, proposals.</li>
  <li><strong>Delivering negative information</strong> — dismissals, criticism, any bad news.</li>
  <li><strong>Stand-up or entertainment speeches</strong> — built on a developed sense of humour.</li>
</ol>
<p>Then find an <strong>original angle</strong>. Avoid huge topics like "love" or "industry"; narrow the scope geographically, chronologically, or by your own opinion. Three formats that reliably work:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Myth-busting</strong> — take a stereotype your community believes and refute it.</li>
  <li><strong>Tips &amp; tricks</strong> — short, very specific recommendations that solve a problem.</li>
  <li><strong>Fuck-up stories</strong> — amusing mishaps and mistakes that taught you something invaluable.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The one job of a speech: stay in memory</h2>
<p>Your brain doesn't fully distinguish reality from imagination. Picture your favourite song; now imagine nails on a chalkboard; now taste a lemon. Your body reacts physically to each. Anything your imagination believes shows up in your body — which is why, if you reach a charismatic mental state, your <a href="/blog/body-language-tips-for-confidence/">body language</a> becomes charismatic too. The talks that stick — the best TEDx speeches — do it by managing <a href="/blog/storytelling-techniques/">storytelling principles</a>, the mechanism that switches on imagination.</p>

<h2>Great speaking starts with listening</h2>
<p>We all know how to talk; few know how to listen, and fewer still how to truly hear. High-level communication begins with attentive listening — and even a monologue is a constant interaction I call <strong>"building connection" with the audience.</strong> When the connection is there, the room reacts to your every grimace and gesture.</p>
<p>Creating it needs one condition above all: <strong>the desire to give.</strong> You must genuinely want to deliver your knowledge, stories and jokes — which means you must believe what you're saying is valuable. Actors face the same problem with material they dislike, and they solve it by finding, in every script, (1) what they like, (2) what they believe in, and (3) what is simply interesting to them. <strong>Never stand in front of an audience without doing that homework</strong> — they'll feel it if you don't care.</p>

<h2>How to develop the skill of reacting</h2>
<h3>Play ping pong</h3>
<p>Table tennis is a brilliant simulator for communication: it teaches you to react instantly. You can't think — you just concentrate on the ball coming at you. In conversation, the "ball" is what your partner just said; miss it and you lose the rally.</p>
<h3>Practise mindfulness</h3>
<p>People speak to you not only with words but with an emotional state you feel immediately — attitude, emphasis, pauses. Care about the other person, forget yourself, and listen for the micro-expressions behind which sit emotions and thoughts that may never be spoken. Mindfulness sharpens that sensitivity.</p>
<h3>Take an improv class</h3>
<p>Improvisation develops spontaneity — the ability to be here and now, which is one of the most important communication skills of all.</p>

<h2>Great speaking engages — it doesn't convince</h2>
<p>People constantly undervalue what they have to say. But the goal of a great talk isn't to convince everyone you're right. The real measure is <strong>how strongly your speech draws people in</strong> — how much it makes them think and feel, how far it lets them into your thinking. Some will decide your world isn't for them, and that's fine. Your job isn't to please the audience; it's to give them the chance to connect with the world you've created — to make your talk an <strong>adventure</strong> that lifts them out of time and place.</p>
<p>You do that with a carefully built combination: valuable insight, stories that fire the imagination, dramatic and entertaining examples, the bit of science the analytical listener craves — plus the confidence and personal gravity with which you say it, and a few surprising techniques almost nobody uses. And the only reason they're rare is that almost nobody bothers — which means they're your opportunity to be remembered.</p>

---
title: "How to Develop Charisma: 5 Ways That Actually Work"
description: "Charisma is built, not born. Five ways to develop it — body language, mental states, shifting your focus, controlling your image, and self-acceptance. From theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">Charisma is a rare blend of inner strength and magnetism that can captivate and inspire whole rooms. The most stubborn myth about it is that you're either born with it or you're not. What's actually innate are traits like determination, courage and capacity for work — and those let you <strong>build</strong> charisma. Here are five ways to do it.</p>

        <h2>1. Non-verbal communication</h2>
        <p>From someone's breathing rhythm alone we can hear whether they just woke up, are angry, or are hiding tears, fatigue or anxiety. Your body emits thousands of unconscious signals a minute, all mirroring your thoughts and feelings — what's in your head shows up in your <a href="/blog/body-language-tips-for-confidence/">body language</a>. So charismatic behaviour has to be born in your conduct and emotional world first; if your inner state is anti-charismatic, you can't hide it for long.</p>

        <h2>2. Mental states</h2>
        <p>If you start from the outside — fixing movements before mental states — it's like bolting pretty balconies onto a ruin: it looks better until the first small tremor brings the whole thing down. So the real first task is to develop the mental states that <em>generate</em> charismatic body language.</p>
        <p>You build a mental state by practising it. The brain keeps forming new connections at any age — the more a pathway is used, the more stable it becomes (Gladwell's "10,000 hours" is the same idea: the Beatles played thousands of hours before they were the Beatles). A famous example from <em>The Charisma Myth</em>: a tired, lonely tourist walking down a grand avenue simply <strong>decided she was a film star</strong> there to shoot a movie. Her back straightened, her shoulders dropped back, her clothes suddenly looked refined — and a stranger she asked for directions said he'd be honoured to walk her there.</p>

        <h2>3. Shift your focus</h2>
        <p>Your source of inner energy grows when you focus on yourself and ask the honest questions: <em>What do I really want to do? What are my talents? What have I done that I'm proud of?</em> Founding a school and speaking publicly every week did this for me — the first year was full of nerves and awkward moments, but I slowly grew the connections that now let me feel completely confident in front of hundreds of people, even as an introvert.</p>
        <p>And kindness can be charismatic too — the Dalai Lama has it without a film star's allure. You can develop "charismatic kindness":</p>
        <ul>
          <li>Meditate for 30 minutes picturing the people you resent — and send them love and forgiveness.</li>
          <li>Find a way to feel "the Christmas feeling" every month — genuinely care about friends and prepare for their birthdays a week ahead.</li>
          <li>Find three good things about a person you want to send warmth to.</li>
          <li>Put yourself in another's place — imagine it's their last day, and you're asked to say a few words about them.</li>
        </ul>

        <h2>4. Control your image</h2>
        <p>Image is the everyday mask we use to adapt to different situations — really, our attitudes taking over the controls. We can <em>perform</em> an image, but charisma is the next level: it isn't performed or acted, it's a flow. You've felt it — you walk into a room and instantly know who's in charge, because their mental state shows in posture, expression and gaze. You become more charismatic when you can switch the right mental state on as needed — adapting to the audience and situation rather than using one setting everywhere makes you far more effective.</p>

        <h2>5. Self-acceptance</h2>
        <p>This is the epicentre. Inner flow is impossible without self-belief, and self-belief is impossible without self-acceptance — and in my experience about <strong>80% of people struggle here.</strong> Self-rejection grows from moments when we decided we weren't worthy. A girl who muddled her lines in a school concert was told by a relative, "How could you fail?" — three decades later, those words still surface every time she's on stage.</p>
        <p>Begin by drawing your "anxiety portrait": the expressions and situations where you don't feel at home in your own skin, the things that throw you off balance — your Achilles heels. Anxiety badly damages charisma; the two are almost opposite states.</p>
        <blockquote>Brené Brown defines shame as "the fear I'm not good enough to be loved."</blockquote>
        <p>Shame hits so hard because it's tied to social rejection — for most of human history, being cast out by the tribe meant death. So remember that uncomfortable emotions are normal, inherited survival instincts. Don't over-dramatise every situation, and when perfectionism flares over a tiny slip, think of all the people who walked the same path before you.</p>

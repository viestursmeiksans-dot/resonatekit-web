---
title: "How to Engage an Audience: 3 Strategies That Work"
description: "Hold any room by speaking to its four psychological types, varying complexity, and shifting persona. Three audience-engagement strategies from theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">Your audience is made of very different psychological types, each making sense of the world in its own way. To hold the whole room, you have to design your talk for all of them. Here are three strategies that multiply your impact.</p>

        <p>Start from the uncomfortable truth one director called "the multi-headed monster": look out from the stage and you see rows of heads, and <strong>every one of them is an egoist.</strong> Each listener is there for themselves — to learn something useful, gain a skill, meet someone, buy or sell. They care about <em>their</em> outcome. Good engagement works with that, not against it.</p>

        <h2>Strategy 1 — Speak to all four audience types</h2>
        <p>Roughly speaking, every room contains four psychological types. Address each and the whole room stays with you:</p>
        <ol>
          <li><strong>The scientific type</strong> understands the world through rational thinking. Give them comparisons, before-and-after, numbers, prices, sizes — anything that establishes value.</li>
          <li><strong>The emotional type</strong> understands the world by identifying with someone else's situation. Give them experiences — stories and examples.</li>
          <li><strong>The "journalist" type</strong> understands by talking it through themselves. Give them a chance to speak — a Q&amp;A, a prompt, a moment to react.</li>
          <li><strong>The visual type</strong> understands through images. Give them pictures, video or a live demonstration.</li>
        </ol>
        <p>Notice how this hands you a structure: build in a stat, a story, a question and a visual, and you've covered everyone. (For the story part, see <a href="/blog/storytelling-techniques/">storytelling techniques</a>.)</p>

        <h2>Strategy 2 — Vary the complexity</h2>
        <p>Your talk should contain episodes pitched at three different levels: roughly a <strong>fourth-grade</strong> level, a <strong>junior-professional</strong> level, and a <strong>master</strong> level. The more complex the content, the more energy it takes to absorb — so the audience needs periodic relief. Keep the introduction and conclusion simple enough to reach everyone (a story is far easier to follow than terminology), and concentrate the harder material in the middle. A nice touch: warn the room that "heavy artillery" is coming, so they can brace for it.</p>

        <h2>Strategy 3 — Shift between three personas</h2>
        <p>Watch skilled political speakers and you'll notice they change persona within a single speech:</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>The strict figure</strong> — talks about red lines, things that won't be tolerated, deadlines that <em>will</em> be met. Purposeful, in control.</li>
          <li><strong>The visionary</strong> — the same person turns slightly dreamy, painting a vivid picture of where things go in 10–20 years. Someone who sees what others can't.</li>
          <li><strong>The grateful one</strong> — finds moments to thank people: a partner, a team, a mentor, even the hard year just passed. Gratitude is powerful because it signals that the speaker values others and isn't trapped in self-admiration — that they're empathetic.</li>
        </ul>
        <p>Moving between these registers keeps the room awake and shows range — a core part of <a href="/blog/how-to-be-a-great-public-speaker/">great public speaking</a>.</p>

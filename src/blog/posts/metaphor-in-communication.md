---
title: "How to Use Metaphor in Communication & Public Speaking"
description: "Metaphor is a shortcut to understanding and memory. How to use metaphors and comparisons to make complex ideas land — from theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">Metaphor is the turbo button of communication. Press it, and an audience grasps a complex idea far faster than any plain explanation could deliver it. It is also one of the most poetic tools you have — a way to fold entertainment, surprise and emotion into a single image. Used well, <strong>metaphor can become the single most powerful weapon in your communication arsenal.</strong></p>

<h2>Metaphor as a bridge</h2>
<p>A metaphor works as a bridge. It connects an abstract, fuzzy idea to something far more concrete and familiar, drawing a parallel with what the listener already knows. Think of the old line "love is a journey." Suddenly an intangible feeling has roads, detours, arrivals and dead ends — things we can picture. That is why metaphor is so often reached for when we try to describe the emotional world: it gives shape to the things that resist plain definition.</p>
<p>When you stand in front of an audience and need to make an unfamiliar concept land, a good metaphor does the heavy lifting for you. Instead of stacking up qualifiers and definitions, you hand the listener one vivid picture and let their imagination finish the work.</p>

<h2>What metaphor actually is</h2>
<p>Metaphor is one of the basic tools of artistic expression. You build it from ordinary parts of speech — <strong>nouns, adjectives and verbs</strong> — but you point them somewhere unexpected. It is the engine of most poetry, and it works just as hard in a speech, a pitch or a story.</p>

<h2>Powerful metaphors borrow familiar characters</h2>
<p>Some of the strongest metaphors lean on characters everyone already carries in their head — figures from films, cartoons and pop culture. The reference does the describing for you in a single beat:</p>
<ul>
  <li>"Two police officers opened my door who looked like Asterix and Obelix."</li>
  <li>"A woman called me who sounded exactly like Marge Simpson."</li>
  <li>"I shuffled into the queue like one of the Minions."</li>
</ul>
<p>Characters stick in our minds not because they behave well, but because they are <strong>recognisable, identifiable and a little unusual.</strong> Even the most likeable of them has bad days and emotional explosions — and that is precisely what makes them memorable.</p>
<p>You can stretch a single comparison into a whole scene, too:</p>
<p>"I felt like Roger Federer climbing out of a time machine into a 1920s gentlemen's tennis tournament. The others wore long white trousers and played with heavy wooden rackets. I had turned up with an ultralight graphite racket, a personal trainer and computer-modelled strategy. We were playing the same game, we were equally sharp — but we were on completely different levels."</p>

<h2>Metaphors can be funny — and endlessly creative</h2>
<p>The best metaphors are vivid, often humorous, and impossible to forget. The one rule that never moves: the metaphor must still do its core job — making the topic or idea clearer. <em>How</em> you do that is up to you and, above all, up to your audience. A few examples of the range:</p>
<ul>
  <li>"I felt like a funeral director who had stopped caring."</li>
  <li>"He looked like a raven that had been left out in the rain."</li>
  <li>"That news landed about as unexpectedly as snow in July."</li>
  <li>"He walked into the room looking like a bear that hadn't slept all winter."</li>
  <li>"Her curls had started to resemble the wild north face of a mountain."</li>
</ul>
<p>When you reach for a comparison, three reliable wells to draw from are:</p>
<ol>
  <li>Film and TV characters</li>
  <li>Well-known actors, musicians or public figures</li>
  <li>Animals and birds</li>
</ol>
<p>If you want to weave metaphor into longer narrative — turning a comparison into a moment of story — it pairs naturally with <a href="/blog/storytelling-techniques/">storytelling techniques</a> that give your material shape and momentum.</p>

<h2>How to build your own metaphors</h2>
<p>A good metaphor rarely arrives by accident. The reliable method is to start from the abstract idea you need to convey, then ask: <em>what does this feel like?</em> Search for a concrete, familiar image from the three wells above — a character, a public figure, or an animal — that shares the same essential quality. The closer that shared quality sits to the heart of your point, the harder the metaphor lands.</p>
<p>A few principles keep your comparisons sharp rather than confusing:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Pick the familiar over the clever.</strong> If half the room won't recognise the reference, the bridge collapses. A metaphor that needs explaining has already failed.</li>
  <li><strong>Make one clear point per image.</strong> Mixed metaphors — two pictures fighting inside one sentence — leave an audience untangling instead of understanding.</li>
  <li><strong>Let the image carry the emotion.</strong> "He looked like a raven left out in the rain" already tells us bedraggled and miserable. You don't need to add the adjectives on top.</li>
  <li><strong>Match the tone to the moment.</strong> A playful comparison lifts a light passage; in a serious one, an out-of-place joke can puncture the whole thing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Build the habit of collecting comparisons as you notice them — in conversation, in films, in the way someone describes their day. Keep a running list. When you next need to make a dry idea vivid, you'll have a stock to reach into instead of grasping for one mid-sentence.</p>

<h2>Metaphor gives your communication flavour</h2>
<p>Metaphors are among the most powerful instruments a speaker has. They compress meaning, trigger emotion and lodge an idea in memory — all at once. The skill is learnable: you build a habit of noticing comparisons, collecting them, and dropping them in at the moment they will land hardest.</p>

<h2>In short</h2>
<p>A plain explanation asks an audience to work. A good metaphor does the work for them — and leaves a picture behind that long outlasts your words. Build the habit of finding the right comparison, and you give every idea you carry a shortcut straight into someone else's understanding.</p>

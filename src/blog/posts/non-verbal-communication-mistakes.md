<p class="lead">Non-verbal communication is the science of stability. Its only job is to make you look <strong>dependable</strong> — and most people ruin it with needless swaying, fidgeting, crossed legs and other parasitic habits. It matters more than the words themselves: we decide whether to keep listening based on whether we trust the speaker, and we don't trust someone who looks unsteady.</p>

<h2>Your pre-speech checklist</h2>
<p>Before you speak — to a room or one-to-one — run this quick check:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Feet.</strong> Firmly planted, as if roots grow into the floor. You are grounded.</li>
  <li><strong>Back.</strong> Spine straight — essential for feeling secure and looking confident.</li>
  <li><strong>Hands.</strong> Palms turned slightly up and open — a semi-active position that signals you're ready to share something valuable.</li>
  <li><strong>Gestures.</strong> Fingers visible. They're the most expressive part of the body and often move without your control — so put them to work deliberately.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The mistakes that signal uncertainty</h2>
<p>Your body language reads as "uncertain" the moment you:</p>
<ol>
  <li>Stand with crossed legs</li>
  <li>Sway your upper body in any way</li>
  <li>Hide your hands behind your back or let them hang lifeless at your sides</li>
  <li>Lower your gaze</li>
  <li>Fidget — playing with pens, hair, earrings, rings, a fidget toy</li>
  <li>Scratch — scalp, hands, fingers</li>
  <li>Pace across the stage non-stop</li>
</ol>

<h2>How to move on stage</h2>
<p>"Stage" is any space where you stand in front of others — and moving well doesn't mean pacing like a caged tiger. Use two positions:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Central position</strong> — stand centre-stage for your introduction and conclusion. It shows you own the talk.</li>
  <li><strong>Decentralised position</strong> — for the body of the talk. Your speech is built from several episodes; deliver each from a different spot. After the intro, take a step or two to the side or forward, stay put while you deliver that episode, then move to another off-centre position for the next.</li>
</ul>

<h2>On camera and seated</h2>
<p>Online, your stability matters just as much. On Zoom or Teams: keep your <strong>feet flat</strong> on the floor, your <strong>spine straight</strong>, and your <strong>fingers visible</strong> in frame. And don't lean back into the chair's backrest — it reads as too relaxed and drains your authority.</p>

<h2>In short</h2>
<p>The body never lies; it's only an instrument reflecting your inner state. So to <em>look</em> confident, you have to <em>be</em> in a confident state and think the way confident people think — then there's nothing for an audience's lie-detector to catch.</p>

title: "How to Overcome Stage Fright & Public Speaking Anxiety"
description: "Why nerves double in front of an audience — and how to fix it. Find the source of your fear, reach a calm focused state, and switch to plain spoken language. From theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">Stress is energy — it's supposed to exist. The problem is <strong>blocking stress</strong>: when anxiety climbs so high you can no longer think, speak or stay calm. Here are three reasons nerves double in front of an audience, and how to work on each: destructive self-criticism, over-complicated language, and lack of preparation.</p>

<h2>First, find where your stress began</h2>
<p>Anxiety is deeply individual. It usually grows from one of three roots:</p>
<ol>
  <li>A <strong>negative experience.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Disbelief in your own abilities</strong> (an inferiority complex).</li>
  <li><strong>Fear of the unknown.</strong></li>
</ol>
<p>Pay special attention to your school years. That's when identity forms, and a single piece of criticism or humiliation can give a young person a reason to close off — the fear of speaking publicly often first appears around age 13–15. Later we see only the symptoms: speakers who rush, who hold uncomfortably long pauses, whose mouths go dry, whose voices crack, who run out of breath, who blush in patches.</p>

<h2>Reach a state of calm and focus</h2>
<p>Professional athletes face stress constantly, and they chase the same thing before competing: deep calm combined with focus. Four things help you get there before a talk or exam:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Drink warm water.</strong> A warm drink removes roughly 30% of anxiety — bring a thermos and keep yourself warm. Avoid coffee; it over-stimulates.</li>
  <li><strong>Put on headphones with music that lifts you.</strong> They double as a way to seal yourself off from other anxious people around you.</li>
  <li><strong>Recall a moment when everything went right.</strong> Close your eyes and relive a specific situation where you nailed it and others praised you — where you were 100% proud of yourself.</li>
  <li><strong>The "master's" stance.</strong> Hands on your hips, held for 2–3 minutes. Feel the confident "owner" gene switch on.</li>
</ul>
<p class="italic">"There is nothing either good or bad, but thinking makes it so." — Hamlet</p>

<h2>Stepping out of your comfort zone is not self-punishment</h2>
<p>It's a powerful tool for growth. Nobody is born afraid of public speaking — we picked the fear up somewhere and believed it. So the work is to find the <strong>source</strong> of your fear, and then take the next step:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Resolve it</strong> if you can — improve your diction, use a little tinted cream so the blushing doesn't show, prepare a plan B/C/D so you always have something to say if you lose your place.</li>
  <li><strong>Accept it</strong> if you can't. Make it part of you, the same way you accept a strong accent or a striking nose. Accept the very expression you most dread — and become its most passionate defender.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Your attitude decides whether it's a tragedy</h2>
<p>Your speech becomes a tragedy only <strong>if your attitude is tragic.</strong> If you face the audience apologising — "there's a nuclear war going on in my head right now" — they'll watch a tragedy too. But you don't have to get stuck on a mistake if you've done the homework. The homework: close your eyes and picture yourself in front of the audience at the exact moment the thing you dread most happens. Now imagine you <em>like</em> that expression — that it's an inseparable part of who you are. Accept your most frightening moment, and it loses its grip.</p>

<h2>Written and spoken language are two different galaxies</h2>
<p>People over-complicate their speech for two reasons: "it's a professional audience, they'll understand," and "I don't want to seem unprofessional." Complicated language is also the second-most-popular way (after slides) to hide your personality.</p>
<p>Here's the paradox: <strong>half of all anxiety disappears the moment you switch to talking in your own words.</strong> People immediately open up and put down the weight, because they stop trying to defend a status or follow some imagined rulebook — and start simply caring that a colleague understands the idea. So why not do that in every talk? Roughly 50% of speaking anxiety is self-inflicted: expectations set too high, responsibility piled too deep, energy spent pretending.</p>

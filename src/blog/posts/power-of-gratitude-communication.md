---
title: "The Power of Gratitude in Communication"
description: "Why gratitude is one of the strongest communication tools you have — and how to use it sincerely. From theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">"Thank you" is a small, almost magical phrase that carries enormous potential — and in the science of communication, that potential is badly underrated. Used well, gratitude disarms the people in front of you, lifts their mood, and leaves an emotional aftertaste that keeps them thinking about you and your message long after you've finished.</p>

<p>There is an unwritten rule of stagecraft: <strong>shape your opening and your closing deliberately</strong>. A good opening earns you the right to be heard for the rest of the talk. A strong ending creates a powerful aftertaste — the audience can stay inside the feeling your speech created for a long time. Gratitude is one of the most reliable ways to build that ending.</p>

<h2>Why gratitude is so powerful</h2>
<p>Gratitude is woven into communication everywhere. Politicians use it to win a little extra goodwill. Candidates use it in interviews to show respect. Children use it to win over their parents. The word itself triggers a positive emotion almost instantly — and it is one of the most effective opening moves in resolving a conflict.</p>
<p><strong>Gratitude works because it disarms.</strong> It softens an audience, lowers their guard, and makes them more receptive to everything that follows. On top of that, it creates an <strong>aftertaste</strong> — an emotional pull that keeps people turning your subject over in their minds well after the room has emptied.</p>
<p>Its second great function is to <strong>lift the audience emotionally</strong>. It is almost always better to end on a rising curve. Give people energy so they leave the room more charged than when they arrived, not more drained. Yet most speakers neglect this entirely: roughly <strong>70% of the time, the conclusion is treated carelessly</strong> or skipped altogether.</p>

<h2>The finale — how to create an aftertaste</h2>
<p>One highly effective way to inspire people at the close is the <strong>"before and after" method</strong>. Describe how things were before a change, then paint the reality that follows it. This dramaturgy works so well that advertising leans on it constantly — and it works just as powerfully from the speaking platform.</p>
<p>A second function of the finale is to <strong>give people a way to keep exploring your topic</strong> — a kind of practical continuation. Treat your talk as a first meeting that invites further encounters. Those "meetings" don't have to be in person; your listener can keep meeting the subject without you in the room. Close by inviting one small next step:</p>
<ul>
  <li>A book or resource recommendation</li>
  <li>Signing up for a free lesson or consultation at a set day and time</li>
  <li>A slide with your email address or phone number</li>
  <li>An invitation to follow your channel so they keep receiving material on the topic</li>
</ul>
<p>Don't forget the <strong>summary</strong>, either. Every talk has a central thesis. State that message in the opening, the middle, and the conclusion — but each time in different words; there's no point repeating a sentence the audience already heard. In the summary you can also restate your structure. That helps people understand and remember what you said: you give them a kind of map of the talk and re-order their priorities, highlighting what mattered most.</p>
<p>A <strong>wish</strong> fits the conclusion organically, because it functions as a farewell — the moment you gently break off contact. Much of your aftertaste is created with this single instrument. Emotion is the stickiest tool a speaker has, and the more sincerely you use it at the end, the stronger the trace your talk leaves behind. In the wish you can:</p>
<ol>
  <li>Thank the audience for their attention, their participation, their questions</li>
  <li>Wish them something connected to your topic</li>
  <li>Voice a rhetorical wish — a hope that something larger might change</li>
  <li>Give them something (for a small group, even a physical gift; for a large one, a book or film recommendation is a gift too)</li>
</ol>

<h2>"Thank you" is the full stop</h2>
<p><strong>Always end your talk with "Thank you."</strong> It is the period at the end of the sentence — the final note of the performance, after which there is no more room for questions, answers or discussion. (All of that belongs <em>before</em> the conclusion.) "Thank you" is also the signal that tells the audience it's time to applaud.</p>
<p>It doesn't sound complicated — and yet more than 70% of presentations still lack a real ending. Saying "thanks for listening" the instant the middle section runs out is not a conclusion; it's throwing away your talk's potential. Invest the time to build a proper close, and you'll feel it pay off. If you want the front end to match, see how to <a href="/blog/how-to-be-a-great-public-speaker/">become a great public speaker</a> from the very first word.</p>

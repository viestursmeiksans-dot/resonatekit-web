<p class="lead">You can architect a system most people in the room could never build — and still lose them in the first ninety seconds of explaining it. That gap isn't a knowledge problem. It's a <strong>presentation problem</strong>, and for software engineers it quietly decides who gets the staff promotion, whose project gets funded, and who gets remembered after the conference. Here is how to fix it, from theatre and opera director Viesturs Meikšāns.</p>

<h2>Do software engineers actually need presentation skills?</h2>
<p>More than almost any other role. The further you go — senior, staff, tech lead, founder — the less your day is about writing code and the more it's about getting other people to understand, trust, and act on your thinking. Sprint reviews, demos, design proposals, incident post-mortems, conference talks, promotion panels, interviews: every one is a presentation. The brutal truth is that two engineers of equal skill will have very different careers if one can make a room <em>get it</em> and the other can't.</p>

<h2>Why engineers lose the room: you present the <em>how</em>, they need the <em>so-what</em></h2>
<p>The default engineering instinct is to explain how the system works — the architecture, the trade-offs, the clever part you're proud of. But your audience (a PM, a director, a customer, a hiring manager) almost never needs the <em>how</em>. They need the <strong>so-what</strong>: what problem this solves, what changed because of it, and what it means for them.</p>
<p>Think of it as inverting the pyramid. You built bottom-up — data model, services, edge cases. You must <strong>present top-down</strong> — impact first, then just enough mechanism to make the impact believable. The implementation detail you find most interesting is usually the part you should cut.</p>

<h2>How to explain technical concepts to a non-technical audience</h2>
<p>This is the single most valuable skill an engineer can build, and it comes up in every senior interview. Three moves:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Lead with one plain sentence.</strong> Before any diagram, say what it does in language your parents would understand. "It cuts checkout failures in half." Then — and only then — open the hood.</li>
  <li><strong>Use one strong analogy, not five.</strong> A cache is a notepad on your desk so you don't walk to the archive every time. A good analogy does more work than a perfect technical definition.</li>
  <li><strong>Translate, don't dumb down.</strong> Respecting your audience means removing jargon, not removing substance. "Eventual consistency" becomes "everyone sees the change within a second or two, not instantly." Same idea, zero jargon.</li>
</ul>
<p>If you over-complicate your language under pressure, that's often nerves talking, not clarity — and there's a fix for that (see <a href="/blog/overcome-stage-fright/">how to overcome stage fright</a>).</p>

<h2>A structure that works for demos, sprint reviews and talks</h2>
<p>Engineers tend to present chronologically — "first I did this, then this." Nobody can follow a chronology of work they didn't do. Use a structure built around the listener instead:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>The problem</strong> — in one sentence, why this mattered.</li>
  <li><strong>What changed</strong> — the before/after, ideally with a number.</li>
  <li><strong>How (briefly)</strong> — the one or two decisions worth knowing.</li>
  <li><strong>What's next / what I need</strong> — the ask, the risk, the decision you want.</li>
</ol>
<p>For a live demo, decide the <em>one</em> thing you want people to remember and build everything around it. A demo that shows five features lands worse than a demo that proves one outcome.</p>

<h2>The delivery part nobody teaches engineers</h2>
<p>You can have the perfect structure and still lose people if your voice trails off, your eyes stay on the screen, and you rush. Delivery is a craft — the same craft actors and directors train for years. The fundamentals that matter most for an engineer:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Calm and focus before you start.</strong> Athletes chase the exact same state before competing. A warm drink, a slow breath, and one clear intention beat any amount of last-minute slide-tweaking.</li>
  <li><strong>Hold attention deliberately.</strong> Pauses, a change of pace, a single rhetorical question — these are tools, not accidents. (More in <a href="/blog/how-to-be-a-great-public-speaker/">how to be a great public speaker</a>.)</li>
  <li><strong>Let your body back you up.</strong> Open posture, hands visible, eyes on faces not on the laptop — it signals confidence even before you've earned the room's trust. (See <a href="/blog/body-language-tips-for-confidence/">body language tips for confidence</a>.)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Where this pays off</h2>
<p>The engineers who can present don't just give better talks — they get believed faster, get their designs approved, pass the "communication" bar in senior interviews, and become the person leadership puts in front of customers and the board. It compounds. The good news: unlike most of engineering, this is a small set of learnable habits, and you can start improving on the very next stand-up.</p>

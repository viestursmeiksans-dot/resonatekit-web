---
title: "Storytelling Techniques: How to Tell a Captivating Story"
description: "The five elements every gripping story needs — hero, chronology, desire, antagonist and turning point — plus how dramatic thinking holds an audience. From theatre director Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">A good story lets listeners identify with the world you create — it turns them from spectators into witnesses living through what you describe. Every gripping story is built from five elements: a <strong>hero</strong>, a <strong>chronology</strong>, something the hero <strong>wants</strong>, an <strong>antagonist</strong>, and a <strong>turning point</strong>.</p>

<p>Master these and your audience listens differently — you start to feel exactly how carefully they're following you. Stories are communication's richest source; learning to tell them teaches you to think dramatically, or cinematically. Crucially, <strong>storytelling is a tool, not an end.</strong> It's a long-term mechanism that clarifies how attention works, so that communication in any form — a pitch, a report, a keynote — becomes denser and more vivid.</p>

<h2>1. The hero</h2>
<p>For a story to work, the audience must follow one main character — it could be you, a client, or a team. Picture a beach with a million footprints, each a person with their own story; the moment you pick out one specific person, the story starts working, because now we can follow them. Establish who the hero is in the first 30 seconds, along with the time and place, and keep developing them — share their thoughts and feelings, and ideally let their views change as the story unfolds.</p>
<ul>
  <li>Give your hero talents and interests — make them human and three-dimensional.</li>
  <li>Describe their physical appearance.</li>
  <li>Your hero might be a client with a problem, a founder chasing an idea, or a manager facing a hard task.</li>
</ul>

<h2>2. Chronology</h2>
<p>Working with time makes a story incredibly engaging — and can rescue weak material. Insert a flashback, create a little misdirection, or split the story into three clear time periods, and suddenly it gains colour and momentum. Think of the introduction and conclusion as a frame that holds the whole picture together. Here's a "hero's journey" framed in time:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Introduction:</strong> From early childhood, Māris dreamed of becoming a doctor, like his father.</li>
  <li><strong>Primary school:</strong> At six he's diagnosed with dyslexia; teachers push for a transfer, classmates mock him.</li>
  <li><strong>Secondary school:</strong> His father reads aloud to help him learn; against the odds, Māris finishes.</li>
  <li><strong>University:</strong> He's admitted to medical school; three years in, his father dies, leaving him alone. He can read, but it takes four times longer than for others — and still he graduates.</li>
  <li><strong>Finale:</strong> Ten years into a successful career as a doctor, he says: "I know my father would be proud of me."</li>
</ul>
<p>Notice two things. The introduction and finale are <strong>synchronised</strong> — the dream we heard at the start is realised at the end, closing the frame. And the middle is organised chronologically across three periods, which instantly helps the listener orient and absorb information.</p>

<h2>3. What the hero wants</h2>
<p>In fishing terms, the hero's desire is the <strong>bait</strong> your listener follows. The more vivid and valuable that bait, the more likely they'll stay to the end — it's why people finish bad films just to see how they turn out. The absence of a clear want is the number-one cause of boring stories. Four questions help you build it:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>What happens if they don't get it?</strong> Ilze, in intensive care after a fall, has one lifeline to pull her out of her lowest point: the climbing expedition she refuses to cancel.</li>
  <li><strong>How long have they wanted it?</strong> A five-year-old who can't yet read decides he'll become a surgeon like his father — and aims at it his whole life.</li>
  <li><strong>Why do they want it?</strong> Fifteen-year-old Aleksandrs sees mountains for the first time and <em>must</em> climb one — nearly dying of thirst on the way.</li>
  <li><strong>What changes when they get it?</strong> A young man with a heart condition enters Iron Man triathlons to prove — to everyone and himself — that he isn't finished.</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. The antagonist</h2>
<p>Great stories run on contrast — chiaroscuro in painting, counterpoint in music. If the hero and their dream are the light, the antagonist is the shadow. The antagonist isn't necessarily a villain; it's the problem or obstacle blocking the hero. Its function is to create <strong>resistance</strong> — and the greater the resistance, the more compelling the story.</p>
<blockquote>Only through the shadows the antagonist casts does your listener get the full picture.</blockquote>
<p>In business stories, antagonists are competitors, bureaucracy, a talent shortage, force majeure, incompetence, fear and doubt, or the loss of a major client. To understand success, you have to talk about the mistakes and failures along the way. The more attention you give the shadow, the stronger the contrast — and the more interesting your story.</p>

<h2>5. The turning point</h2>
<p>Imagine driving through an unfamiliar city when the wind has knocked over a road sign. You miss the turn, trust it'll be fine, and end up going the wrong way down a one-way street. The turning point is the precise moment your hero is forced in a new, unplanned direction — a disaster, a lost client, a diagnosis, a chance meeting, falling in love.</p>
<p>This is the most-skipped element in all of storytelling, and it shouldn't be. Its definition is simple: <strong>nothing will ever be the same again.</strong> Everything before it was prologue — context laid down so the turning point lands like an explosion. In a real sense, your story <em>begins</em> here.</p>

<h2>Don't collect tricks — change how you think</h2>
<p>Capturing attention with individual tricks is exciting, but you can't lean on tricks alone through long, serious meetings and conferences. One client ran technical seminars up to six hours long and wanted new attention-holding tricks — his audience would switch off after two hours. The answer wasn't more tricks; it was to change his <strong>thinking style</strong>. After learning to think dramatically, he felt in control of his material, could read how the room felt and respond — a mechanism that works even in a twelve-hour seminar.</p>

<h2>In conclusion</h2>
<p>Storytelling is only an instrument — a form you fill with content, ideas and material about your concept or product. That doesn't happen in a day, so start a <strong>storytelling notebook</strong>: collect raw material for your business stories, jot down creative flashes, and answer the deep question of <em>why</em> you do what you do. Stories are magical — but only combined with your other communication tools do you become genuinely unstoppable and memorable.</p>

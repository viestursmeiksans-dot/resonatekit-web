---
title: "The Art of Asking Good Questions"
description: "Better questions get better answers — and build trust, influence and rapport. A theatre director's guide to the art of asking questions. From Viesturs Meikšāns."
---
<p class="lead">Asking good questions is the foundation of human communication. If you want to be liked, open people up with questions that let them talk and share. If you want to close an important deal, let the other side tell you what's stopping them from reaching their goals — then shape your solution around exactly that. And if you want to give feedback that inspires rather than wounds, you do it with questions that let the other person arrive at the right action on their own. It is one of the most useful skills you can develop, and almost nobody is taught it.</p>

<h2>Why questions are so powerful</h2>
<p>Every conversation is a kind of jungle. People come to you with different habits, moods and intentions, and every exchange is quietly a game of influence — from passive aggression to flattery. When you actually need something from a conversation — a result, an agreement, a change of mind — it's like walking into that jungle without a map, hunting for a treasure chest. The only way forward is to ask, everywhere and of everyone, for <strong>directions</strong>. Nobody hands you the map unprompted. And "treasure chest" means one thing to you and something completely different to them, so the path is littered with misunderstandings.</p>
<p>Questions cut through the fog. The person across from you is the only door into their reality — a world with its own culture, its own values, its own quiet sources of pride. Good questions are how you get invited in. As George Bernard Shaw put it, the single biggest problem in communication is the illusion that it has taken place. We arrive assuming we already know what the other person wants, and that assumption is where most disagreements are born.</p>

<h2>Open the conversation, don't close it</h2>
<p>The first thing to understand is the difference between two kinds of questions:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Closed questions</strong> hunt for a specific, factual answer. Once you get it, the conversation is over. These are functional, transactional questions — useful, but they don't build anything.</li>
  <li><strong>Open questions</strong> don't expect a tidy reply. They probe and explore, like a curious researcher who senses there's something to find and won't stop at the first obstacle, the first "no", the first dead end.</li>
</ul>
<p>A useful guest mindset helps here. When you visit someone's home, your eyes and ears work harder — you're more alert, more careful not to break anything. Adopt the same posture in any high-stakes conversation: you are the guest, intensifying your attention, never provoking, never trying to win the argument. The old Socratic ratio is a good target: you speak twenty percent of the time, the other side eighty. Your job is to help them think and to help them reach their own decisions.</p>

<h2>The questions to avoid</h2>
<p>Bad questions are expensive. The price is lost motivation, a stalled discussion, a relationship that quietly cools. Watch out for these:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Leading questions</strong> with the answer already baked in. The other person feels manipulated, nudged somewhere they didn't choose.</li>
  <li><strong>Knowledge tests.</strong> Questions are meant to explore, not to check whether someone knows the right answer.</li>
  <li><strong>Questions that make someone feel small.</strong> Don't load a question with jargon the other person might not understand — they may be too embarrassed to ask, and they'll lose face.</li>
  <li><strong>Advice or a solution with a question mark glued to the end.</strong> "Have you thought about…?" or "How about we…?" is not a question. It's a suggestion in disguise.</li>
</ul>
<p>One more pattern worth retiring: ending a pitch with "Do you have any questions?" In a negotiation that's practically an invitation for the other side to pile on objections and seize control. If you must, ask instead: "What's the most important thing you'd like me to answer right now?"</p>

<h2>Swap "why" for "what" and "how"</h2>
<p>Negotiation expert Chris Voss recommends building questions around two words — <strong>"what"</strong> and <strong>"how"</strong> — and treating <strong>"why"</strong> like a hot object you handle with tongs. "Why" easily reads as an accusation; tone alone can turn it into a reproach. The fix is simple:</p>
<ul>
  <li>"Why did you do that?" becomes <strong>"What were you trying to achieve?"</strong></li>
  <li>"Why do you think this is a good idea?" becomes <strong>"What led you to choose this one?"</strong></li>
  <li>"Why do you always procrastinate?" becomes <strong>"How will you get this done?"</strong></li>
  <li>"Does this look like something you'd like?" becomes <strong>"How does this look to you?"</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>A "how" question, in particular, asks for help and pulls the other person in. The deeper point: you don't persuade people by pushing your conclusion onto them. The best way to ride a horse is in the direction it's already going. Persuasion isn't forcing someone to believe you — it's helping them stop disbelieving, so they reach your idea as if it were their own.</p>

<h2>The eight types of questions</h2>
<p>Once you're in motion, you have a whole toolkit to draw on. A few of the most useful types:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Demystifying questions.</strong> The most valuable category. They open up territory you didn't even know existed: "Could you walk me through how you see this, so we focus on what's most valuable to you?" They keep feeding you new options.</li>
  <li><strong>"What's the real obstacle?"</strong> People often hand you the wrong problem — a symptom, not the cause. Sharpen the question: "What's the biggest obstacle?" invites vague answers; "What's the <em>real</em> obstacle <em>for you</em>?" forces one specific person to name one precise truth.</li>
  <li><strong>"And what else?"</strong> Deceptively simple, endlessly productive. Ask it three to five times, varying the wording, before you rush in with advice. It surfaces options neither of you had considered.</li>
  <li><strong>Emotional questions.</strong> Where there's emotion, there's energy. "How does that affect you?" or "It sounds like that matters a lot to you" invites people to open up about what truly drives them.</li>
  <li><strong>Negative-scenario questions.</strong> "What's the worst that happens if nothing changes?" People fear loss far more than they value gain, so framing the cost of inaction is powerful.</li>
  <li><strong>Time-and-deadline questions.</strong> "Why now? What happens if the deadline slips?" Link a need to a timeframe and it becomes urgent.</li>
  <li><strong>Clarifying questions.</strong> When someone uses a slippery word like "growth", ask what it means to them — market share? revenue? team development? Vague words breed conflict.</li>
  <li><strong>"Coach me" questions.</strong> "If you were in my position, how would you handle this?" You hand the other person a little control and invite them to step into your shoes.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The discipline of silence</h2>
<p>Here's the rule almost everyone breaks: <strong>after you ask a question, stay silent.</strong> Count to seven if you have to. The urge to fill the gap — to give advice, to leap into solution mode, to show off your brilliant idea before the other person has finished — is overwhelming. Resist it. The most valuable thing someone tells you usually comes in the pause you were tempted to interrupt.</p>
<p>Whoever asks the questions controls the conversation. As Voltaire said, judge a person by the questions they ask, not the answers they give. So when you're at home tonight, don't ask your child what they learned at school — ask them what good question they asked today.</p>

<h2>In short</h2>
<p>Curiosity changes everything: your tone shifts, your attitude softens, and people sense it. Seek to understand, not to win — you never win a deal by winning the argument. If you genuinely believe you can help someone, you'll work to grasp their real problem so you can solve it. The art of asking questions isn't about interrogating. It's about inspiring.</p>
